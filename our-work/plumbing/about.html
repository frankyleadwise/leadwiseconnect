About Honest Work

A plumbing company built differently.

We started Honest Work because we were tired of watching homeowners get sold plumbing services they didn't need. Fifteen years later, we're still doing it the way our granddads would've.

I got my first plumbing apprenticeship at seventeen. My granddad Pete Delaney ran a little one-man plumbing shop out of Seminole Heights — my dad had done a stint in it before going into electrical, but I was the one who stuck. Pete taught me how to sweat copper, how to talk to customers, and how to know the difference between a repair that matters and a repair that just pads the bill. He died in 2006. I still think about him on every job.

After his shop closed, I spent twelve years working for the big plumbing chains in the Bay — the ones you see on TV with the flashy trucks and the smiling techs. And what I saw inside those companies broke me. Technicians were paid on commission, which meant every single call — even a simple flapper replacement — came with a pitch for a $3,500 "system upgrade." Techs who didn't hit upsell targets got fired. Customers were treated like leads, not neighbors.

"A plumber is supposed to fix what's broken. Not invent what's not."

In 2011 I left. I cashed out my 401(k), bought a used Ford van, printed my license number on the side, and started Honest Work. The name was a statement of intent. Every tech we hire is paid hourly, not commission. Every plumber we dispatch is licensed — no apprentices running calls solo, ever. And every quote is flat-rate, on paper, before we start.

Fifteen years later, we have six licensed plumbers on staff, two apprentices in training, and a zero-turnover culture. Everyone on my crew has been with me at least four years. A few were with me from day one. The master plumber who showed up at your house this morning answers to me, not to a commission spreadsheet.

We won't ever be the biggest plumbing company in Tampa. That's not the goal. The goal is to be the one your neighbors tell you to call — and that's the one thing we've always been.

— Mike Delaney
